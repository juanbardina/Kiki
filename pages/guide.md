### USER GUIDE - Navigating the Archive

This digital collection preserves the ephemeral "street art" and DIY publications of the American punk scene from **1980 to 1992**. Use this guide to make the most of the research tools provided.

---

#### **1. Exploring the Visuals**
* **Browse:** The primary gallery where you can see all 3,000+ items. You can use the "Sort" feature to organize posters by date or title.
* **Subjects:** We have tagged items by cultural and political themes. This is the fastest way to find posters related to specific movements like "Anti-Reaganism," "Animal Rights," or "Straight Edge."

#### **2. Specialized Content: Zines**
* **The Zines Section:** Unlike one-page concert flyers, the Zines are multi-page documents. Clicking on a Zine entry will allow you to explore the editorial "voice" of the era—interviews, scene reports, and essays that defined the era's rhetoric.

#### **3. Geospatial Research: Locations & Maps**
* **Locations:** This filters the collection by specific cities and legendary venues (e.g., *9:30 Club* in DC or *Mabuhay Gardens* in SF).
* **Map View:** Use the interactive map to see the geographic density of the punk movement. This helps visualize how the "guerilla street marketing" was distributed across different urban hubs.

#### **4. Temporal Evolution: Timeline**
* **The Timeline:** This tool allows you to track the visual shift in punk aesthetics. You can watch as the jagged, high-contrast Xerox style of the early 80s transitions into the more complex graphic designs of the early 90s.

#### **5. Technical Data for Researchers**
* **Data Tab:** For digital historians, the **Data** page provides the metadata in CSV and JSON formats. This is useful if you wish to perform your own computational analysis or mapping of the 1980–1992 punk era.
